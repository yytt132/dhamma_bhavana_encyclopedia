# Bhavana

First drafted: 2026-06-01

`Bhavana` is often translated as "meditation," but this translation is too narrow. In Buddhist contexts, the term more broadly means cultivation, development, or bringing something into being. It can refer to the cultivation of mental qualities, the development of a path, or a disciplined mode of practice embedded in doctrine, ethics, and training.

## 1. The Problem of Translation

The English word "meditation" often suggests a discrete contemplative technique. `Bhavana` can include such techniques, but it also names a broader process of cultivation. A source-aware encyclopedia should therefore avoid treating `bhavana` as equivalent to modern generic meditation.

## 2. Cultivation Rather Than Technique

As a category of cultivation, `bhavana` may include stabilizing attention, developing insight, cultivating loving-kindness, memorizing or reflecting on teachings, and bringing wholesome states into being. The concept is best understood as a situated activity: a practitioner, within a tradition or training context, cultivates a quality or insight by using a method on an object for a goal.

## 3. Relation to Samatha and Vipassana

Two major Buddhist modes of cultivation are commonly distinguished:

- `Samatha`: calming, stabilizing, or settling the mind.
- `Vipassana` or `vipasyana`: insight, investigation, or seeing clearly.

These should be related to `bhavana`, but not collapsed into it. Traditions differ on whether they are paired, sequenced, pedagogically separated, or interpreted as aspects of one path.

## 4. Source-Aware Disambiguation

The same surface term may appear in early Buddhist, Abhidharma, Mahayana, Vajrayana, modern mindfulness, and self-development contexts. Each use should be tagged by tradition, source, method, object, goal, and claim type.
